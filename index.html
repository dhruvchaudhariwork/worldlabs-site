Methodology

One prompt in. A verdict out.

Built like an eval, not a review roundup. Three steps, no vibes.

1

Generate

Every tool gets the identical prompt spec — genre, mechanics, and a deliberate twist ("a 2D puzzle-platformer where gravity flips on jump"). No cherry-picking, no re-rolls.

2

Play blind

Vetted game developers play each game in the browser with tool names stripped from every surface. A session timer enforces a minimum play time before the scoring form unlocks.

3

Judge

Seven rubric dimensions scored 1–7 against fixed anchors, a required written critique, a prioritized fix list, and head-to-head A/B comparisons. Submissions lock on submit.

Why trust it

Rigor a marketing page can't fake

Blind mode

No logos, no tells

Tool identities are hidden from every expert-facing surface for the entire round — pages, URLs, and API responses included. Experts judge the game in front of them, not the brand behind it.

Timed sessions

Minimum play time, enforced

The scoring form stays locked until the expert has actually played — at least 60% of the target session length, tracked server-side so a refresh can't reset the clock.

Vetted panel

Shipped-title experts only

Level designers, systems designers, UX specialists, QA and playtest leads — every panelist is vetted on shipped credits and years in the industry. Judgment you can't crowdsource.

Reliability

Two raters, disagreement flagged

Every game is scored independently by at least two experts. If their overall means diverge by 2.0 or more, the game is flagged and a third rater is assigned. Consensus is earned, not assumed.

Broken builds

"Could not play" is data too

When a generated game doesn't run, it's excluded from score averages — but each tool's broken rate is published right on the leaderboard. Fragility is part of the result.

Pairwise

Head-to-head, same prompt

For every prompt, cross-tool pairs are judged A/B in randomized order: which is the better game, at what confidence, and why — in writing. Win rates come from real comparisons.

Data

Export-clean by design

Every judgment lands in a stable JSON schema with full context: rubric scores, critiques, fix lists, pairwise choices, time played. The benchmark is also a dataset.

The rubric

Seven dimensions a playtest lead would actually use

Each dimension is scored 1–7 against fixed anchors, so a 4 from one expert means the same thing as a 4 from another.

01 Prompt adherence Does the game match what the prompt asked for? 1 = unrelated · 4 = loosely related · 7 = faithful, including the twist
02 Mechanics coherence Do the rules make sense and interact sensibly? 1 = contradictory, broken rules · 7 = a coherent system with meaningful interactions
03 Fun Did you want to keep playing? 1 = a chore · 4 = mildly diverting · 7 = genuinely engaging
04 Pacing & difficulty Does challenge ramp sensibly? Any dead spots or spikes? 1 = flat or chaotic · 7 = a deliberate curve
05 Game feel Responsiveness, feedback, "juice" — inputs, animation, sound cues. 1 = dead, laggy · 7 = crisp and satisfying
06 Onboarding Could a new player figure out what to do without instructions? 1 = lost immediately · 7 = teaches through play
07 Completeness Does it feel like a whole playable thing, or a fragment? 1 = broken fragment · 7 = complete loop with win/lose state

Every score ships with a required written critique and a top-3 fix list, ordered by impact.
